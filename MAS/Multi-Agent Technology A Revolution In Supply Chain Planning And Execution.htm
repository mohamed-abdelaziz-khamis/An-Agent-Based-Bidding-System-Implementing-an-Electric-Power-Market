Multi-Agent Technology: A Revolution In Supply Chain Planning
And Execution
History has shown that the deployment of complex software to manage the extremely intricate
task of managing the supply chain does not work. It is for this reason that two methodologies
continue to dominate this sector - the use of batch processing and the continued reliance on
manual planning and execution. However, demands for increased efficiency and
responsiveness mean that supply chain pioneers are looking into technologies that can work in
real-time, automate processes and above all are simple to deploy and use.
Traditional enterprise software will not be able to meet these demands. For example, the most
obvious contender would be a rules-based system. On the face of it such a system has the
real-time capability and the ability to automate processes. However, the sheer complexity of
translating the real world into a comprehensive set of what/if statements that can deal with
every possible eventuality and connotation is not only a daunting task, but infeasible in size
and scale. It is highly unlikely that such a system would be practical as it the rules would in
effect to be too rigid and require a lot of manual adjustment and maintenance. Furthermore,
business rules software does not allow you to trace backwards to understand the final result.
Instead, IT logistics pioneers are already turning to multi-agent technology. As well as meeting
the fundamental requirements of automation and real-time responsiveness, the software
allows operations managers to plan and replan within a real world environment. It therefore
removes the necessity for batch processing and provides the capability to react dynamically to
change and, crucially, to resolve any conflicts. The very nature of the system also means that it
is neither complex in size nor use and web-based Java technology ensures ease of
integration.
An overview of multi-agent technology
It is only within the last year that multi-agent technology has successfully made the transition
from the realm of academia to commercial deployment. Interestingly, this has also coincided
with a major science-fiction bestseller by acclaimed Jurassic Park author, Michael Crichton,
who has based his book “Prey” around the technology. Continued growth of the commercial
deployment of this technology is expected in coming months with the logistics sector
trailblazing the way.
The reason for this is because multi-agent technology only truly distinguishes itself from other
existing enterprise software components when dealing with such complex environments as a
supply chain. Unlike traditional hierarchical software designs, multi-agent technology employs

It is important to note that this is by no means the first commercial application of agent

As such, multi-agent system solutions are similar to virtual markets, like a stock market or
commodities market, in that every resource is represented through a single software agent.
These agents are uniquely configured to capture all relevant information to the resource, such
as constraints, the decision-making authority, time, space, geography and so on. The agents
then interact with each other within the virtual market to negotiate the optimum solution. This is
done by analysing the situation, making decisions, communicating and negotiating with other
agents to resolve conflicts, then rendering these decisions to the system and its users.
As agents are intelligent and adaptive, very complex automated resource allocation becomes
possible with minimal programming. A single virtual market can be running several different
strategies simultaneously, making this an exceptional approach to resource allocation.
The rise of the Ontology
The agents purpose and the environment within which they work is provided through the
Ontology. This data comes from external sources and the agents are created within this pre-
defined environment which represents the real and comprehensive simulation of the world.
Although not unfamiliar with the supply chain and logistics sector, multi-agent software is one
of the first technologies to make full use of the ontology concept. The ontology represent a new
way to structure knowledge and is an optimum way to tap into the vast reams of data that
companies are now capturing, storing and managing. Importantly, it also allows individuals to
work together within an environment where all their knowledge has been captured and
represented. In this way they are able to create and manipulate their best ideas to solve
business problems.
The ontology is a well-structured knowledge base represented as a semantic network of object
classes, their roles, relations between them, parameters, processes, strategies and so on. The
user is able to influence the agents through the ontology.
As this is separate from the system code, the non-technical user can do this in real-time
without any interruption to the system. This allows businesses to plan time and resources
accurately and quickly.
Essentially, therefore, the ontology allows the agents to work with data and knowledge sourced
from the real world. This knowledge is a collective store of the company's knowledge and is
fine-tuned to provide best practice. Once accurately defined, and through ongoing refinements,

Key benefits of multi-agent technology
The key characteristics of multi-agent technology provide several benefits over traditional
large, hierarchical programmes. While some systems may share some of these benefits they
are all intrinsic to the nature of multi-agent technology.
Autonomy
Each agent within the system is designed to operate autonomously. As such, multi-agent
systems do not wait for instructions and are capable of reacting to any change in a situation
without being prompted. While this makes multi-agent software intrinsically flexible, this is
within the parameters set by the operator with the agents' domain-specific knowledge
refreshed from the ontology without any need for the system to be shut down. This means that
multi-agent systems are capable of working 24/7 with knock-on benefits in terms of saving
time, money and effort.
Intelligence
Particularly within the supply chain, resource limitations make finding an optimal solution
difficult if not seemingly impossible. Due to the fact that multi-agent technology consists of a
network of small agents, the way in which the system approaches a resource problem or
conflict is both unique and dynamic. The intelligent software agents can negotiate with other
agents representing resources and demands. In addition, the agents compete and cooperate
to make a decision that is best for all participants of the network, under the current dynamic
circumstances. This means that should a conflict arise, the agents will attempt to work around
it and provide a best-fit solution that works. This level of responsiveness is not possible within
other sequential systems which, when faced with conflict, would be unable to provide a
solution or more critically, could crash.
Pro-Activity
Software agents interact with the system users proactively, representing available resources
and demands. Whenever parts of the schedule become infeasible, certain decisions need to
be made by the users who communicated with the agents by email or SMS.
High Performance
High performance and scalability is an extremely important requirement. Multi-agent software
meets this need by using hundreds of thousands of software agents running concurrently on a
single server, exchanging up to 10,000 messages in a second. A typical system would be able
to provide for web applications with one to two seconds of reaction time for ten to twelve users
in parallel. This allows the system to work and react in real-time.
Low Cost Maintenance
A large proportion of software maintenance costs are associated with the software being hard
coded within the system. This means that any changes require expert technical knowledge in
the required programming language and are extremely time-consuming. Within Magenta's
multi-agent technology, the domain-specific knowledge of the system, stored in the ontology, is

Use of multi-agent technology within the supply chain and logistics sector
The logistics and supply chain execution sector is an excellent focus of deployment for multi-
agent technology. Within this sector, operators are constantly faced with uncertainty, change
and conflict that makes planning and execution very challenging and labour intensive attempts
to automate these processes have had limited effectiveness as the system is simply unable to
cope with real world difficulties. While rules-based systems have delivered benefits compared
to manual operations, multi-agent technology offers the opportunity to release even more value
and increased efficiency.
As such multi-agent software deals with the conundrum that as the real world does not work as
a batch process, so the software must reflect this. Rather than continually looking for a perfect
solution, the multi-agent software will search for equilibrium. In other words, a solution that
works rather than necessarily being empirically correct. In this way the software is able to
update a plan in real-time.
The strength of the multi-agent technology is that it models the real world and reaches
solutions based on this environment. This means that logistics managers can plan and replan
in real-time, which mitigates the risk of cascade failure in other words, the problem logistics
operators face when successive daily plans fall behind schedule, creating cumulative delays
as the week progresses. Crucially it also enables accurate modelling of the network. This
allows planners to avoid potential problems by using this simulated environment.
Rules-based systems are not able to handle volatility and real-time accurately enough
because they force processes into a rigid, linear and centralised method of solving problems.
However, multi-agent technology models the complexity of the real world and embraces the
conflicts that are natural in distributed business operations.
The multi-agent technology provides a high level of granularity within the planning and
scheduling process. The system is able to reoptimise a plan or process based around even the
most minor change. For example, to work most efficiently, it may be deemed that a soap
factory should produce 1000kg of soap a day. In a normal planning system, all the constraints
would be mapped and a plan produced to allow the factory to meet this output requirement.
However, this is then ignoring order requirements. For example, it may be that in any given

Commercial deployment
The first commercial deployments of multi-agent technology are underway which will
demonstrate how logistics and supply chain operators are using the technology to give them
cutting edge agility. They will show how some of the most archaic paper-based, manually
calculated operations are finally being consigned to the dustbin because of the technology's
ability to work in real-time and in the real world.
Whether it is hauliers, tanker operators or railway engineering teams, multi-agent technology
has the ability to dramatically improve efficiency and enhance the competitive edge. By
employing the latest in web-based Java technology, customers will be able to deploy the multi-
agent software directly onto their legacy systems.
The first company to adopt multi-agent technology is one of the largest oil tanker pools in the
world. This company is deploying multi-agent technology from Magenta to schedule its fleet in
real-time. The multi-agent software will enable dynamic deployment of ships-to-cargo for the
crude oil tanker fleet. In the highly volatile oil market, where prices fluctuate around the world
extremely quickly, the multi-agent technology will give a crucial competitive edge to ensure the
maximum profitability for its vessels.
The multi-agent technology examines potential cargoes in the marketplace, matches the most
profitable ones to vessels and then selects the best route for that ship to follow to its
destination port. Further, unlike traditional rules-based systems, the multi-agent technology will
respond to real world changes impacting a schedule and replan the most advantageous
schedule at that point in time.
Within this system, the multi-agent technology will work by allocating a single agent to each
element of the business network. These agents will then work collectively to find the best fit
between supply and demand to meet the goals of individual vessels as well as the strategy of
the entire ship pool. Therefore, when a new cargo appears, agents will be created within the
database and contain all the data about that cargo for example price, size and origin. The
agents will then negotiate within the virtual market to decide the optimum way for it to be
collected and delivered. The agents will do this by competing to find the best solution between
supply and demand by comparing alternative routes, vessels, ports, prices and even weather
conditions. This data and the defining concepts upon which the agents base their decisions are

As is necessary within today's globally interdependent business environment, the multi-agent
technology allows the logistics operator to manage a network of systems, whether within the
enterprise or among suppliers, customers and partners. Despite the claims of ERP vendors
and those of other major software applications, this is simply not possible with traditional
applications. Any attempt to build such a system would be extremely bespoke, inflexible and
involve a lot of safety margins as people would still not be able to plan accurately.
Safety margins and 'work arounds' are the bane of a logistics operator even though they are
an everyday compromise. To summarise, a work around is where the actual implementation of
a schedule is only achievable if artificial contingencies are built in. For example, to ensure a
delivery will meet the half hour timeslot, the plan builds in a one hour safety margin. Therefore
if the delivery arrives without any delays, it will actually be an hour early.
Multi-agent technology dramatically reduces such inefficiencies because it is able to
dynamically reschedule in real-time. Crucially, it also allows operators to work backwards from
where they are in the plan to replan. Current systems do not allow you to do this. However,
multi-agent software allows the plan to be rerun as it was actually put in practice and for the
operator to run any combination of what-if scenarios to further refine future plans. Given that
the average logistics operation wastes between 10 and 30% of its operating costs on such
inefficiencies, the potential savings are considerable.
The future for multi-agent technology
The ultimate future of this cutting edge technology lies within the realms of truly intelligent
agents that have natural language functionality, the ability to tolerate input errors and even
process these errors themselves. Such enhancements will provide incredible levels of
responsiveness that will make the complex task of logistics planning and execution
dramatically simpler.
However, as with all emergent technologies, there are some challenges that must be
overcome. Traditional issues such as security and the lack of any industry standards or
toolsets are obvious hurdles that will be naturally dealt with in the fullness of time. A specific
challenge of multi-agent systems is maintaining high performance in an environment where the
agents are designed to be both intelligent and autonomous.
The risk is that the agents themselves may pursue non-mandated tasks or get stuck in loops. It
must be remembered that while this may not necessarily affect the outcome in terms of finding
optimum solutions for the logistics operation, it will burn up processing speed.
One immediate danger is that to impose controls on the agents may lead the developer to
overburden the agent with overly complex programming. The result will be a fat agent which
will not be able to keep pace nor be dynamic enough to provide real-time working. At the end
of the day, multi-agent technology works by reducing complexity and at the most fundamental
level this can only be achieved if the agent programme is kept as simple as possible.

The solutions to these problems are mainly found within the way in which the multi-agent

The potential for multi-agent technology is enormous. Whatever the scale or complexity of the
supply-chain, the technology has the potential to provide enormous gains in efficiency and RoI.
Pioneers will find themselves maintaining or dramatically enhancing their competitive edge as
they reduce fulfilment failures. But it is the reduction in complexity that makes multi-agent
technology such an attractive proposition to logistics managers.
